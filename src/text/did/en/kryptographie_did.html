CRYPTOGRAPHY
Hello! I am Alice and this is my friend Bob. Maybe you have already met us while learning about the secret communication. Both of us are experts in it, thanks to the jealous Eve, who is constantly spying on us. But since we use cryptography, it's not that easy anymore!
Cryptography (ancient kryptós = "hidden, secret" and gráphein = "write") is the science of encrypting information. It includes a variety of encryptions that you can find in the margin column. However, they all have something in common: All encryptions have the task of ensuring the secrecy, authentication or integrity of a message. These are all pretty complicated words!

Bob and I have experienced them all firsthand. In the following scenarios, I am the "sender" and send Bob, the "receiver" a message to arrange a meeting place.

Scenario 1 - Secrecy
In this scenario, I (Alice) would like to send a message to my friend Bob to agree where we will meet. Eva, however, rummages in his mailbox as usual! If she reads this message, she might show up at the rendezvous and play a trick on us. So I use secrecy, so that the message for Eva is impossible or difficult to understand.

Scenario 2 - Authentication
I need to send Bob a message again. Eva has come up with something new. She wants to send a message to Bob too, but pretending to be Alice. Now Bob has two letters and does not know what to do! So I need proof of identity or authentication for my letters so that Bob can check which message actually came from me.

Scenario 3 - Integrity
Again, I (Alice) send my friend Bob a message. Eva picks up the message in the mailbox, and changes it. That's how Bob and I manage to never meet each other! The message must therefore clearly retain its integrity - that means inviolability.


So these are the goals of encryption. But what is such an encryption at all and how do you define it? Perhaps you remember the terms that Bob introduced in the chapter on CRYPTOLOGY. Here is the table again:
Term Abbreviation Description
Alphabet A a finite set of characters (eg A = {a, b, c}).
Cardinality |A| The length (width) of A. This is the number of characters in A (eg | A | = 3).
Plain Text M A simple string "over" A. This means that it only consists of characters of the alphabet.
Key K A string (Eg. "key").
Encoding E Method which generates the cipher text from M and K.
Cipher Text C An unreadable string (Eg. C#&DFDFDharf3413b##$@).
Decryption D Procedure for recovering the plain text.

Here, the encryption E was described as a method . In fact, they are also called Cryptographic Methods. Such a method includes an Algorithm and a Protocol.
The Cryptographic Algorithm

A cryptographic algorithm is really nothing more than a mathematical function, ie a calculation rule. We can imagine that bit like a machine. In this machine you can insert a string, the plaintext M, and get back the cipher text. Mathematically, it is written like this:
E(M) = C
Here E is the machine, ie the cryptographic method. In the parenthesis (M) the "input" and C as the "Output". Decryption works in the same way:
D(C)=M
In order for the decryption to be really clear, both functions need an important feature: Injectivity. This means that two different inputs in E () can not have the same result. Only then:
D(E(M))=C
If you still need explanations of all these mathematical terms, press MATHEMATICS in the margin column.

But what is the difference between cryptography and other forms of secret communication, as well as codings or languages? Let's imagine the situation.



Alice sends me a message. We symbolize the algorithm with a box that can be opened and closed according to a specific procedure. The message is in this box. Again Eva goes after the postman and gets the box. Although she does not know how to open the box, she can go to the library and get information about the algorithm. Once she has figured out how our algorithm works, she can read our message undisturbed. And because she knows our algorithm now, she will be able to read all our messages in the future. That would be terrible! This is exactly the problem when using simple languages.
The solution? Quite simply: our box needs a key. Now, although Eva knows how to open the box, namely plug in key and turn, she can not perform this algorithm because she does not have the key! So the problem is solved and we have a SOLUTION.
With encryption one can observe two types. There are symmetric encryptions. Alice and I have the same key. For asymmetric encryptions we have two different keys. How is that possible? By clicking on the two topics in the margin column, you can learn more about it.
